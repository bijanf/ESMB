\chapter{The Ocean: Overturning and the Thermohaline Circulation}
\label{chap:ch08_ocean_overturning}

% --- local symbols (minimal; not in the shared preamble) ---
\providecommand{\vthc}{\vv_{\mathrm{thc}}}          % thermohaline overturning velocity
\providecommand{\kvel}{k_{\mathrm{vel}}}            % overturning-strength gain
\providecommand{\drho}{\Delta\dens}                 % subpolar-minus-subtropical density contrast
\providecommand{\dS}{\Delta\Salt}                   % subpolar-minus-subtropical salinity contrast
\providecommand{\hgain}{\gamma_{\mathrm{S}}}        % haline_gain
\providecommand{\bS}{\beta_{\mathrm{S}}}            % haline contraction coefficient

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is the global ocean's
single most consequential dynamical mode: a basin-scale conveyor that carries
warm, salty surface water poleward, sinks it in the subpolar North Atlantic to
form North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW), and returns it equatorward at depth.
Its existence and strength depend on a competition between thermal and haline
buoyancy forcing, and \citet{stommel1961} showed in a famous two-box model that
this competition admits \emph{multiple stable equilibria} -- a strong, salt-driven
``on'' state and a weak, fresh-capped ``off'' state. \citet{marotzke1991}
sharpened this into the salt-advection feedback that underlies the modern picture
of AMOC tipping. This chapter documents how \model{} represents that physics, and
the chapter is deliberately honest about what the representation is: an
\emph{interim, mechanistic closure}, not a prognostic-momentum overturning.

\section{Why a closure is needed}

\model{}'s ocean (Chapters~\ref{chap:ch04_ocean_equations}--\ref{chap:ch06_ocean_numerics})
diagnoses horizontal velocities from the density field via a thermal-wind /
geostrophic balance. That balance is \emph{local in latitude}: it relates the
vertical shear of $\uvec$ to the local horizontal density gradient, but it does
not constrain the basin-integrated, depth-overturning streamfunction that reaches
$26.5^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$. The practical symptom is that the sensitivity of the
diagnosed AMOC to a subpolar buoyancy perturbation is essentially zero,
\begin{equation}
  \pd{\Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}(26.5^{\circ}\mathrm{N})}{\Salt_{\mathrm{subpolar}}} \approx 0,
  \label{eq:dpsi-zero}
\end{equation}
so the diagnostic ocean carries no coherent overturning response to forcing and
cannot host an AMOC bifurcation. The prognostic-momentum core that would close
this honestly (a variable-coefficient JEBAR barotropic-vorticity solver plus a
$\partial\uvec/\partial t$ equation) is the subject of Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}
and is future work. In the interim we install the cheapest \emph{differentiable}
change that makes Eq.~\eqref{eq:dpsi-zero} nonzero and sign-correct, so that the
tipping experiments of Chapter~\ref{chap:ch17_amoc_tipping} can be run at all.

\begin{remark}
The closure prescribes the vertical \emph{shape} of the overturning cell (as a
box model does) but makes its \emph{strength} and, crucially, its \emph{sensitivity}
to density physical. It is set to zero by $\kvel=0$, which recovers the pure
diagnostic ocean.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/overturning.py}{module docstring: rationale and scope}
\end{remark}

\section{A density-driven, depth-integral-zero overturning velocity}

The closure adds a meridional overturning velocity $\vthc(z,\lat,\lon)$ confined
to the Atlantic basin. Two design constraints fix its form.

\paragraph{(i) It must carry no net meridional transport.}
The model's barotropic corrector (Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core})
removes any depth-uniform meridional flow per latitude, so a closure velocity
with a nonzero depth integral would simply be annihilated. We therefore require
\begin{equation}
  \int_{-\dep}^{0} \vthc \dd z = 0
  \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
  \sum_{k} \vthc^{(k)}\,\Delta z_k = 0 ,
  \label{eq:depthzero}
\end{equation}
which guarantees $\vthc$ drives only \emph{overturning} (and the associated
vertical velocity $\ww$ and tracer transport), never net throughflow.

\paragraph{(ii) Its strength must scale with the subpolar buoyancy excess.}
Deep-water formation is gated by how much denser the subpolar convection region
is than the subtropics. \model{} reads an upper-ocean density contrast
\begin{equation}
  \drho \;=\; \langle\dens\rangle_{\mathrm{subpolar}} - \langle\dens\rangle_{\mathrm{subtropical}},
  \label{eq:drho}
\end{equation}
where $\langle\cdot\rangle$ denotes an area- and depth-weighted mean over Atlantic
ocean cells in a latitude band ($45$--$65^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$ for subpolar,
$10$--$30^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$ for subtropical) and over the convection layer
$z \ge -h_{\mathrm{c}}$. A salinity contrast $\dS$ is read the same way.

\subsection{Vertical shape}

Let the depth-mid coordinates be $z_k$ and define the upper-limb indicator
$\mathbb{1}_{\mathrm{up}}(z) = 1$ for $z \ge -h_{\mathrm{up}}$ and $0$ below, with
$h_{\mathrm{up}}$ the limb thickness (\code{upper\_depth\_m}, default $1100\,\mathrm{m}$).
Writing $d_{\mathrm{up}}=\sum_k \mathbb{1}_{\mathrm{up}}(z_k)\,\Delta z_k$ and
$d_{\mathrm{dn}}=\dep-d_{\mathrm{up}}$, the dimensionless shape
\begin{equation}
  G(z) \;=\; \mathbb{1}_{\mathrm{up}}(z)
            \;-\;\bigl(1-\mathbb{1}_{\mathrm{up}}(z)\bigr)\,\frac{d_{\mathrm{up}}}{d_{\mathrm{dn}}}
  \label{eq:Gshape}
\end{equation}
is $+1$ in the upper limb and negative below, and is constructed so that
$\sum_k G(z_k)\,\Delta z_k = 0$ exactly, satisfying Eq.~\eqref{eq:depthzero}.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/overturning.py}{\code{thc\_overturning\_velocity}: \code{G = upper - (1-upper)*(up\_d/dn\_d)}}

\subsection{Strength, contrast and the haline amplification}

The overturning amplitude is a smooth, one-sided ($\ge 0$) function of a
haline-amplified contrast,
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{contrast} &= \drho + (\hgain - 1)\,\bS\,\dS , \label{eq:contrast}\\
  A &= \kvel \,\softplus\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{contrast}}{\Delta\dens_{\mathrm{scale}}}\right),
        \qquad \softplus(x) = \ln(1+e^{x}), \label{eq:amp}
\end{align}
with $\bS \approx 0.78\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}\,psu^{-1}}$ the haline contraction
coefficient $\partial\dens/\partial\Salt$ of the equation of state near
$35\,\mathrm{psu}$, $\Delta\dens_{\mathrm{scale}}$ the contrast scale
(\code{drho\_scale}, default $0.1\,\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$), and $\kvel$ the velocity
gain. The full overturning velocity is then
\begin{equation}
  \vthc(z,\lat,\lon) \;=\; A\;G(z)\;\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{Atl}}(\lat,\lon)\,\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{ocn}},
  \label{eq:vthc}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{Atl}}$ is the Atlantic basin mask
($280$--$360^{\circ}\mathrm{E}\cup 0$--$20^{\circ}\mathrm{E}$, $34^{\circ}\mathrm{S}$--$80^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$)
and $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{ocn}}$ the wet-cell mask.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/overturning.py}{\code{amp = k\_vel*jax.nn.softplus(contrast/drho\_scale)}; \code{v\_thc = amp*G*atl*maskC}}

The $\softplus$ rectifier replaces a hard $\max(0,\cdot)$: it is everywhere
differentiable, so $\partial A/\partial(\text{state})$ exists for the gradient-based
calibration and bifurcation diagnostics that motivate the whole model, yet it
saturates to a linear ramp for large positive contrast (denser subpolar water
$\Rightarrow$ stronger AMOC) and decays smoothly toward zero for negative contrast
(a fresh/warm subpolar cap $\Rightarrow$ collapsed cell).

\begin{example}[Reading the knobs]
With $\hgain=1$ the haline term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:contrast} vanishes and the
strength depends on the \emph{total} density contrast only -- the conservative
default. Setting $\kvel=0$ removes the closure entirely. Raising $\kvel$ scales
the cell strength linearly in the linear regime of $\softplus$; $\Delta\dens_{\mathrm{scale}}$
sets how sharply strength turns on with contrast.
\end{example}

\subsection{Two decoupled depth scales}

A subtlety that matters for tipping: the depth over which the cell \emph{flows}
($h_{\mathrm{up}}$, the limb thickness in Eq.~\eqref{eq:Gshape}) is decoupled
from the depth over which the gating \emph{contrast} is read
($h_{\mathrm{c}}=$~\code{contrast\_depth\_m}, default equal to $h_{\mathrm{up}}$).
Physically, deep-water formation responds to the buoyancy of the \emph{upper few
hundred metres} of the convection region, so a shallow $h_{\mathrm{c}}\sim300\,\mathrm{m}$
is the correct choice -- and it is also the numerically consequential one. A
surface-trapped freshwater anomaly averaged over $0$--$1100\,\mathrm{m}$ is diluted
roughly $20\times$ and barely equilibrates within a decadal hold; averaged over
$0$--$300\,\mathrm{m}$ it is diluted only $\sim6\times$ and flushes in a few years.
The shallow contrast layer thus gives a surface hosing far more leverage on the
contrast, which is exactly the control variable in the bifurcation study.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/overturning.py}{\code{upper\_depth\_m} (shape) vs \code{contrast\_depth\_m} (gating)}

\section{The salt-advection feedback (\texorpdfstring{$\hgain$}{haline\_gain})}

The closure is not merely diagnostic: because $\vthc$ advects the prognostic
salinity field, it carries salt poleward in its upper limb. A stronger AMOC
therefore makes the subpolar Atlantic saltier and denser, which by
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:contrast}--\eqref{eq:amp} strengthens the AMOC further. This is
precisely the \citet{stommel1961}/\citet{marotzke1991} salt-advection feedback,
realised here through the model's own tracer transport rather than imposed.

The loop gain is tunable through $\hgain$ (\code{haline\_gain}). When $\hgain>1$
the contrast in Eq.~\eqref{eq:contrast} weights the subpolar salinity excess
\emph{above} its raw density contribution, $\bS\dS$, by the extra factor
$(\hgain-1)\bS\dS$. This amplifies the overturning's sensitivity to the salinity
contrast specifically, raising the feedback-loop gain above unity and turning the
monostable diagnostic AMOC into a \emph{bistable, tip-capable} one. The bifurcation
that results -- the hysteresis loop swept by a slowly varied freshwater hosing -- is
the object of study in Chapter~\ref{chap:ch17_amoc_tipping}.

\begin{remark}
Because the feedback runs through the model's prognostic salinity, the sign of
$\partial\Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}/\partial\Salt_{\mathrm{subpolar}}$ is now positive
(saltier subpolar $\Rightarrow$ stronger AMOC), repairing
Eq.~\eqref{eq:dpsi-zero}. $\hgain$ controls only \emph{how strong} that feedback
is, i.e.\ whether the system is monostable or bistable.
\end{remark}

\section{Subpolar freshwater hosing}

The standard AMOC-bifurcation forcing is a freshwater ``hosing'' of the subpolar
North Atlantic surface. \model{} implements it as a virtual surface-salinity
tendency. Spreading a freshwater volume flux of $Q_{\mathrm{fw}} = H_{\mathrm{Sv}}\times10^{6}\,\mathrm{m^{3}\,s^{-1}}$
($H_{\mathrm{Sv}}$ in Sverdrups) uniformly over the subpolar Atlantic surface
region $\mathcal{R}$ (band $45$--$65^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$, area $\mathcal{A}=\sum_{\mathcal{R}}\Delta x\,\Delta y$,
surface-layer thickness $\Delta z_0$) dilutes a reference salinity $\Salt_{\mathrm{ref}}=35\,\mathrm{psu}$,
giving the virtual salt-removal rate
\begin{equation}
  \left.\pd{\Salt}{t}\right|_{\mathrm{hose}}
   = -\,\frac{Q_{\mathrm{fw}}\,\Salt_{\mathrm{ref}}}{\mathcal{A}\,\Delta z_0}
     \quad \text{on } \mathcal{R},\qquad 0 \text{ elsewhere.}
  \label{eq:hosing}
\end{equation}
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/overturning.py}{\code{subpolar\_hosing\_salt\_tendency}: \code{-(hosing\_sv*1e6*s\_ref)/(area*dz0)}}

Freshening lowers the subpolar density, weakens the contrast $\drho$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:drho}, and so weakens the AMOC through Eq.~\eqref{eq:amp}; the
salt-advection feedback then either damps the perturbation (monostable regime) or
amplifies it past a saddle-node (bistable regime). Eq.~\eqref{eq:hosing} is
differentiable in $H_{\mathrm{Sv}}$ -- so $\partial\Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}/\partial H_{\mathrm{Sv}}$
is available by automatic differentiation -- and $H_{\mathrm{Sv}}=0$ is an exact
no-op.

\section{Diagnosing the overturning streamfunction}

The overturning is reported as the meridional streamfunction in depth--latitude
space. For the global MOC,
\begin{equation}
  \Psi(\lat,z) \;=\; \int_{-\dep}^{z}\!\!\int_{\lon_{\mathrm w}}^{\lon_{\mathrm e}}
                     \vv(\lon,\lat,z')\,\dd x \dd z',
  \label{eq:moc}
\end{equation}
discretised as a longitudinal transport sum followed by a vertical cumulative sum,
$\Psi_{jk} = \sum_{i}\vv_{ijk}\,\Delta x_j$ accumulated over $z$, reported in
Sverdrups ($1\Sv = 10^{6}\,\mathrm{m^{3}\,s^{-1}}$).
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/diagnostics.py}{\code{compute\_moc}}

\subsection{The Atlantic basin section and the barotropic correction}

For the AMOC the integral in Eq.~\eqref{eq:moc} is restricted to wet Atlantic
cells. But the model's coarse longitude-box ``Atlantic'' is open at its southern
edge and to throughflow, so the raw $\vv$ contains a large depth-uniform
(barotropic) component -- the wind-driven gyre / ACC passing \emph{through} the box --
which would otherwise swamp the diagnostic at the level of $\sim-200\Sv$. Two
steps fix this: (i) restrict the section to \code{atlantic\_mask}~$\cap$~\code{ocean\_mask};
and (ii) remove the section depth-mean transport at each latitude,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{V}(\lat) = \frac{1}{\dep}\sum_{k}\Bigl(\sum_i \vv_{ijk}\,\Delta x_j\Bigr)\Delta z_k,
  \qquad
  V'_{jk} = \Bigl(\sum_i \vv_{ijk}\Delta x_j\Bigr) - \widehat{V}(\lat),
  \label{eq:barotropic}
\end{equation}
so the corrected section carries zero net meridional transport and the
streamfunction closes at the bottom. The depth-integral-zero construction of
$\vthc$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:depthzero} is what lets the closure survive this
correction intact.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/diagnostics.py}{\code{compute\_amoc}, \code{remove\_barotropic}}

\subsection{Sign convention}

The AMOC streamfunction is accumulated as a \emph{positive} cumulative sum,
\begin{equation}
  \Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}(\lat,z) = \frac{1}{10^{6}}\sum_{k'\le k} V'_{jk'}\,\Delta z_{k'} \;[\Sv],
  \label{eq:amoc-sign}
\end{equation}
following the RAPID convention: \textbf{positive $\Psi$ is the AMOC}, i.e.\ northward
NADW in the upper limb with an equatorward deep return. The upper-cell strength at
$26.5^{\circ}\mathrm{N}$ is then $\max_z \Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}$ and the AABW lower
cell is $\min_z \Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}$.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/diagnostics.py}{\code{amoc\_sv = cumsum(...)}; \code{upper\_cell\_26N = max}, \code{lower\_cell\_26N = min}}

\begin{remark}[A real sign bug, fixed]
An earlier version negated Eq.~\eqref{eq:amoc-sign} ($\Psi=-\!\sum$). With that
sign a physically correct overturning cell is entirely non-positive, so
$\max_z\Psi$ scored a genuine $\sim15\Sv$ AMOC as $0\Sv$ (and an \emph{anti}-AMOC
as large positive). Injecting a clean $15\Sv$ cell confirms it:
$\max(-\!\operatorname{cumsum})=0.0$ while $\max(+\!\operatorname{cumsum})=+15.0$.
This sign error -- not only the diagnostic-velocity ocean -- is why the AMOC once
read $\sim0$ / ``incoherent.'' (See \code{tests/test\_amoc\_metric.py}.)
\end{remark}

\section{Honest limitations}

This closure prescribes the cell's vertical structure and confines it to a fixed
longitude box; it is therefore not a substitute for prognostic overturning. The
strength's \emph{magnitude} is a tuning of $\kvel$ rather than an emergent balance,
and the basin geometry is crude at T31. What the closure does buy -- and all it
claims to buy -- is a differentiable, sign-correct, physically motivated dependence
of the overturning on the subpolar buoyancy contrast, with a tunable salt-advection
feedback. That is exactly the minimal ingredient needed to study AMOC bistability
and tipping, which we take up in Chapter~\ref{chap:ch17_amoc_tipping}.

\begin{exercise}[Turning the feedback on]
Using \code{thc\_overturning\_velocity}, hold a fixed density/salinity field and
sweep \code{haline\_gain} $\in\{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ at fixed $\kvel$. For each, plot
the overturning amplitude $A$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:amp}) as a function of an imposed
subpolar salinity contrast $\dS$. Identify which $\hgain$ first produces a
loop-gain $>1$ (slope of $A$ vs $\dS$ exceeding the contrast restoring rate) and
relate it to the onset of bistability.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Why depth-integral-zero?]
Construct a test overturning velocity $\vthc = A\,G(z)$ on the model grid with
$G$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:Gshape}, and a second one with $G\to G+c$ for a constant
$c\ne0$. Pass both through \code{compute\_amoc} with \code{remove\_barotropic=True}.
Explain quantitatively why the barotropic correction (Eq.~\ref{eq:barotropic})
leaves the first cell intact but annihilates the constant offset of the second.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[The hosing transfer function]
Apply \code{subpolar\_hosing\_salt\_tendency} for $H_{\mathrm{Sv}}\in[0,0.3]\Sv$
in a multi-year integration with the closure active ($\kvel>0$,
$\code{contrast\_depth\_m}=300\,\mathrm{m}$). Plot the equilibrated AMOC upper-cell
strength (Eq.~\ref{eq:amoc-sign}) against $H_{\mathrm{Sv}}$. Then repeat with
$\code{contrast\_depth\_m}=1100\,\mathrm{m}$ and explain, using the dilution
argument of Section~3.4, why the shallow contrast layer gives the hosing far more
leverage.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Verifying the sign convention by differentiation]
Compute $\partial\,\Psi_{\mathrm{AMOC}}(26.5^{\circ}\mathrm{N})/\partial H_{\mathrm{Sv}}$
by automatic differentiation through \code{subpolar\_hosing\_salt\_tendency} and a
short ocean step. Confirm the sign is negative (freshening weakens the AMOC) and
contrast it with the result one would get under the buggy $\Psi=-\!\operatorname{cumsum}$
convention of the final remark.
\end{exercise}
