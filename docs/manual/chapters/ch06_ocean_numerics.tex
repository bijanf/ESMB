\chapter{The Ocean: Numerics and Solvers}\label{chap:ch06_ocean_numerics}

% --- local symbols (kept minimal; used only in this chapter) -------------------
\providecommand{\rdrag}{r}              % linear (Rayleigh) drag coefficient
\providecommand{\Hcol}{H_{\mathrm{col}}} % wet-column depth
\providecommand{\softp}{\operatorname{softplus}}

Chapter~\ref{chap:ch04_ocean_equations} laid out the continuous primitive
equations the ocean component aspires to solve, and Chapter~\ref{chap:ch05_ocean_mixing}
filled in the subgrid closures (isopycnal/Gent--McWilliams mixing, vertical
diffusivity). This chapter is about the \emph{discretisation} that actually runs:
how one ocean time step is assembled in code, which terms are stepped
prognostically and which are diagnosed, and the linear-algebra machinery (a
differentiable conjugate-gradient elliptic solver) underneath it.

The central, load-bearing fact of this chapter --- stated plainly so the reader is
never misled --- is that in the current model \emph{only the tracers are
prognostic}. Temperature $\Temp$, salinity $\Salt$, and dissolved inorganic carbon
are advanced in time by Runge--Kutta integration of an advection--diffusion
equation. The velocity field $(\uu,\vv,\ww)$ is \emph{diagnosed} every step from
the density and wind-stress fields via geostrophy, thermal wind, a flat-bottom
barotropic streamfunction, and continuity. There is no prognostic momentum
equation: the model never integrates $\partial\uvec/\partial t$. This is a
deliberate, honest simplification with real consequences for the overturning
circulation, and it is precisely what motivates the prognostic-momentum core
developed in Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}. The whole step lives in
one \jax-jitted function.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{\code{step\_ocean} --- the entire ocean time step}

\section{Anatomy of one ocean time step}

A single call to \code{step\_ocean} executes, in order:
(1)~update density from the equation of state;
(2)~build the diagnostic horizontal velocity from wind stress (barotropic) plus
thermal wind (baroclinic), then correct its net transport;
(3)~diagnose the vertical velocity from continuity under a rigid lid;
(4)~advance the tracers one step with RK4;
(5)~apply salinity sponges, hard physical clips, and a global-mean conservation
renormalisation. Steps~(2)--(3) are pure diagnostics recomputed from scratch each
step; only step~(4) carries state forward in time. The ocean time step is
$\Delta t = 900\,\mathrm{s}$ (15 minutes).
\codref{chronos\_esm/config.py}{\code{DT\_OCEAN}}

\subsection{Equation of state}
Density is a linear function of potential temperature and salinity,
\begin{equation}
  \dens(\Temp,\Salt) = \densz - \alpha\,(\Temp-\Temp_0) + \beta_S\,(\Salt-\Salt_0),
  \label{eq:eos}
\end{equation}
with $\densz = 1025\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$, thermal expansion $\alpha = 0.2\
\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}\,K^{-1}}$, haline contraction $\beta_S = 0.8\
\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}\,psu^{-1}}$, and reference state $(\Temp_0,\Salt_0)=(283.15\
\mathrm{K},\,35\ \mathrm{psu})$. This is the simplest seawater EOS; it omits the
pressure dependence and the nonlinear cabbeling/thermobaricity terms of a full
TEOS-10 formulation \citep{griffies2004}, but it keeps the density a smooth,
exactly differentiable function of the prognostic tracers --- which matters because
the entire diagnostic velocity, and hence the gradient $\partial(\text{AMOC})/
\partial(\text{density})$, flows back through \eqref{eq:eos}.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{\code{equation\_of\_state}}

\section{The diagnostic velocity field}

The horizontal velocity is split, in the classical way, into a depth-independent
\emph{barotropic} part and a depth-varying \emph{baroclinic} part,
$\uvec = \uvec_{\mathrm{bt}} + \uvec_{\mathrm{bc}}$.

\subsection{Barotropic streamfunction: a Stommel-style balance}

For the barotropic flow the model does not integrate a momentum equation; it solves
a steady, linearly damped vorticity balance in the spirit of \citet{stommel1961}.
Taking the curl of a wind-driven, bottom-drag-balanced depth-integrated momentum
equation and writing the transport as a streamfunction $\psib$, with
$U_{\mathrm{bt}}=-\partial\psib/\partial y$, $V_{\mathrm{bt}}=\partial\psib/\partial x$,
gives a Poisson problem
\begin{equation}
  \lap\psib \;=\; \frac{(\curl\boldsymbol{\tau})_z}{\densz\,\dep\,r_{\mathrm{bt}}},
  \qquad
  (\curl\boldsymbol{\tau})_z = \pd{\tau_y}{x} - \pd{\tau_x}{y},
  \label{eq:psi-rhs}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\tau}=(\tau_x,\tau_y)$ is the surface wind stress, $\dep=\sum_k
\Delta z_k$ the total depth, and $r_{\mathrm{bt}}=1/(25\times86400\,\mathrm{s})$ a
tuned 25-day linear drag. The wind-stress curl is evaluated with centred
differences (\code{jnp.roll}), and \eqref{eq:psi-rhs} is solved by the
conjugate-gradient Poisson solver of \S\ref{sec:cg} with the previous step's
$\psib$ as a warm start. The barotropic velocity is then recovered by centred
differencing of $\psib$:
\begin{equation}
  U_{\mathrm{bt}} = -\frac{\psib_{j+1}-\psib_{j-1}}{2\,\Delta y},
  \qquad
  V_{\mathrm{bt}} = \frac{\psib_{i+1}-\psib_{i-1}}{2\,\Delta x}.
\end{equation}
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{step 3, \code{solve\_poisson\_2d} call + $\psib$ differencing}

\begin{remark}[Flat bottom]
The Laplacian in \eqref{eq:psi-rhs} is constant-coefficient: it implicitly assumes a
\emph{flat bottom} (depth $\dep$ pulled out of the divergence). Real bathymetry
enters the tracer transport through wet-cell masks but \emph{not} into the
streamfunction operator. This mismatch is the source of the spurious net transport
corrected in \S\ref{sec:corrector}, and the variable-coefficient
$\diver(\dep^{-1}\grad\psib)$ operator that fixes it properly is exactly the
JEBAR-type solver discussed in \S\ref{sec:varcoef} and built upon in
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Baroclinic flow: hydrostatic pressure and damped thermal wind}

The baroclinic shear comes from the horizontal density gradient. The model first
forms the hydrostatic pressure anomaly by integrating the density anomaly down from
the surface,
\begin{equation}
  \pres'(z) = \grav \int_0^{z}\big(\dens-\densz\big)\dd z'
  \;\approx\; \grav\sum_{k'\le k}\big(\dens_{k'}-\densz\big)\,\Delta z_{k'},
  \label{eq:hydro}
\end{equation}
a cumulative sum over levels (\code{jnp.cumsum}). Rather than the bare geostrophic
balance (which is singular at the equator where $\Cor\to 0$), the model uses a
\emph{Rayleigh-damped} geostrophy --- the steady, linearly-drag-balanced momentum
equation $\Cor\,\kvec\times\uvec + \rdrag\,\uvec = -\densz^{-1}\grad\pres'$ ---
which inverts to
\begin{align}
  \uu_{\mathrm{geo}} &= -\,\frac{\rdrag\,\partial_x\pres' + \Cor\,\partial_y\pres'}
                                {\densz\,(\Cor^2+\rdrag^2)},\\
  \vv_{\mathrm{geo}} &= \phantom{-}\frac{\Cor\,\partial_x\pres' - \rdrag\,\partial_y\pres'}
                                {\densz\,(\Cor^2+\rdrag^2)},
  \label{eq:damped-geo}
\end{align}
with $\Cor = 2\Om\sin\lat$. The denominator $\Cor^2+\rdrag^2$ never vanishes, so the
equatorial singularity of pure geostrophy is removed by the drag $\rdrag$; the
Coriolis parameter is additionally floored at $|\Cor|=10^{-5}\,\mathrm{s^{-1}}$ for
safety. To split off the depth-mean (which the barotropic streamfunction already
carries), the wet-column average is subtracted:
\begin{equation}
  \uvec_{\mathrm{bc}} = \big(\uvec_{\mathrm{geo}} - \overline{\uvec}_{\mathrm{geo}}\big)\,m_C,
  \qquad
  \overline{\uvec}_{\mathrm{geo}} = \frac{1}{\Hcol}\sum_k \uvec_{\mathrm{geo},k}\,\Delta z_k\,m_{C,k},
  \label{eq:bc-anom}
\end{equation}
where $m_C\in\{0,1\}$ is the 3-D wet-cell mask (MITgcm-style \code{hFac} rule: a
cell face is open only if both adjacent centres are wet) and $\Hcol=\sum_k\Delta
z_k\,m_{C,k}$ the per-column wet depth. The full diagnostic velocity is
$\uvec = \uvec_{\mathrm{bt}} + \uvec_{\mathrm{bc}}$, masked to wet cells.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{step 4, hydrostatic pressure + damped thermal wind + wet-column anomaly}

\begin{remark}[Why this is only diagnostic, and why it matters]
Equations~\eqref{eq:damped-geo}--\eqref{eq:bc-anom} give a velocity that is
\emph{algebraically slaved} to the instantaneous density field. There is no time
derivative of momentum, no advection of momentum, no JEBAR/bottom-pressure-torque
coupling between topography and the flow. The thermal-wind shear is local in
latitude and carries essentially no \emph{net} meridional overturning to a fixed
section: a direct test gives $\partial(\text{AMOC at }26.5^\circ\mathrm{N})/
\partial(\text{subpolar salt})\approx 0$. Closing a realistic Atlantic overturning
therefore needs an extra mechanism (\S\ref{sec:thc} and
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch08_ocean_overturning}) and, ultimately, prognostic momentum
(Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Per-latitude barotropic net-transport corrector}\label{sec:corrector}

A flat-bottom velocity streamfunction applied over \emph{variable} bathymetry is
inconsistent: the depth-integrated meridional transport is $V_{\mathrm{bt}}\Hcol$,
but $\partial(\psib\Hcol)/\partial x \neq \Hcol\,\partial\psib/\partial x$ when
$\Hcol$ varies in $x$. The leftover divergence shows up as a large spurious
\emph{net} meridional transport --- order $\pm 350\Sv$ at some latitudes, where a
closed ocean basin requires $\approx 0$. With that much fictitious net flow, no
clean overturning cell can survive. As a first correction (it is honestly only a
first one), the model removes the net transport latitude by latitude: subtract a
latitude-uniform meridional velocity so that the zonally- and vertically-integrated
transport vanishes at every $\lat$,
\begin{equation}
  \vv \;\leftarrow\; \Big(\vv - \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{x,z}\vv\,\Delta z\,m_C}
                               {\displaystyle\sum_{x,z}\Delta z\,m_C}\Big)\,m_C ,
  \label{eq:corrector}
\end{equation}
($\Delta x$ is uniform in $x$, so it cancels in the ratio). This drives the net
per-latitude transport to $\approx 0\Sv$ and is stable in long runs. It is a
\emph{patch}, not a cure: a proper per-basin / C-grid transport streamfunction (the
variable-coefficient solve of \S\ref{sec:varcoef}) is the fuller fix.

\subsection{Thermohaline overturning closure}\label{sec:thc}

Because the diagnosed thermal wind carries no net overturning, a depth-integral-zero
Atlantic overturning velocity $\vv_{\mathrm{thc}}$ is added as an interim,
box-model-style closure in the spirit of \citet{marotzke1991}. Its strength scales
with the subpolar-minus-subtropical upper-ocean density contrast, so denser
(colder/saltier) subpolar water drives a stronger cell:
\begin{equation}
  \vv_{\mathrm{thc}} = k_{\mathrm{vel}}\;\softp\!\Big(\frac{\Delta\dens_{\mathrm{c}}}{\Delta\dens_{\mathrm{scale}}}\Big)\,G(z)\,m_{\mathrm{Atl}},
  \qquad
  \Delta\dens_{\mathrm{c}} = \Delta\dens + (\gamma_h-1)\,\beta_S\,\Delta\Salt,
  \label{eq:thc}
\end{equation}
where $G(z)$ is a fixed vertical shape that is $+1$ in the upper limb and negative
below, with $\sum_k G_k\Delta z_k = 0$ (so the closure carries \emph{no} net
transport and survives the corrector \eqref{eq:corrector}). The softplus keeps
$\vv_{\mathrm{thc}}\ge 0$ and differentiable; $\gamma_h$ (\code{haline\_gain})
amplifies the haline term to lift the salt-advection feedback gain above unity,
making the AMOC bistable for the tipping experiments of
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch17_amoc_tipping}. This finally gives density a working pathway
to the overturning ($\partial(\text{AMOC})/\partial(\text{density})\neq 0$,
sign-correct). The closure and its feedback are derived in full in
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch08_ocean_overturning}.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/overturning.py}{\code{thc\_overturning\_velocity}}

\section{Vertical velocity from continuity (rigid lid)}\label{sec:w}

An overturning cell only transports heat and salt if $\ww$ actually advects
tracers, so $\ww$ must be consistent with the same horizontal flow that does the
advecting (including the Gent--McWilliams bolus velocity, $\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}} =
\uvec + \uvec_{\mathrm{bolus}}$). The hydrostatic continuity equation,
\begin{equation}
  \pd{\ww}{z} = -\diver\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}} = -\Big(\pd{\uu_{\mathrm{eff}}}{x}+\pd{\vv_{\mathrm{eff}}}{y}\Big),
  \label{eq:continuity}
\end{equation}
is integrated \emph{upward} from a rigid floor ($\ww=0$ at the bottom). For the
column to be closed at \emph{both} ends --- a rigid lid means no tracer is advected
through the ocean surface either --- the depth-mean horizontal divergence is removed
first (a rigid-lid projection):
\begin{equation}
  \diver\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}} \;\leftarrow\; \diver\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}}
       - \frac{1}{\Hcol}\sum_k (\diver\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}})_k\,\Delta z_k ,
  \qquad
  \ww_{\mathrm{if},k} = -\!\!\sum_{k'\ge k}(\diver\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}})_{k'}\,\Delta z_{k'} .
  \label{eq:w-project}
\end{equation}
After projection the discrete 3-D flow $(\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}},\ww)$ is exactly
nondivergent, so the \emph{advective}-form vertical advection conserves the tracer
mean. This projection is not cosmetic: without it the residual column divergence
piles into the surface cell and leaks the global mean at roughly $+0.25\
\mathrm{K\,yr^{-1}}$ of spurious heating, because the advective form (unlike flux
form) has no compensating compression term to absorb it. The interface vertical
velocity $\ww_{\mathrm{if}}$ is masked so that interior interfaces are open only
where both adjacent centres are wet, and the surface and floor interfaces are closed.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{``Vertical velocity for tracer advection'' block}

\begin{remark}[Two vertical velocities]
The model computes $\ww$ twice. The version above ($\ww_{\mathrm{if}}$ from
$\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}}$) is the one that advects tracers in the RK4. A separate,
purely diagnostic $\ww$ is also stored in the returned state, computed from the
final $(\uu,\vv)$ with the same upward continuity integral but \emph{without} the
rigid-lid projection and with no $\cos\lat$ metric term. The stored field is for
inspection only; the tracer transport uses the projected one.
\end{remark}

\section{Tracer time stepping: RK4 advection--diffusion}

The prognostic tracers $\Temp,\Salt,\mathrm{DIC}$ obey
\begin{equation}
  \Dt{C} = \underbrace{-\,\uvec_{\mathrm{eff}}\!\cdot\!\grad_h C}_{\text{horiz.\ advection}}
           \;-\; \ww\,\pd{C}{z}
           \;+\; \grad_h\!\cdot\!(\kappa_h\grad_h C)
           \;+\; \pd{}{z}\!\Big(\kappa_z\,\pd{C}{z}\Big)
           \;+\; F_{\mathrm{surf}}\,\delta_{k0},
  \label{eq:tracer}
\end{equation}
written so that all terms appear as a single tendency $\mathcal{T}(C)$. The
right-hand side is built from four masked operators:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Horizontal advection} is first-order \emph{upwind}: the upstream
difference is selected by the sign of $\uu_{\mathrm{eff}},\vv_{\mathrm{eff}}$
(\code{jnp.where}). Closed faces return the centre value (zero gradient), so no flux
crosses a coast.
\item \textbf{Horizontal diffusion} is a 5-point Laplacian with coefficient
$\kappa_h$ in the interior and a large $2\times10^4\,\mathrm{m^2\,s^{-1}}$ near the
poles (a numerical sponge against polar grid-convergence noise; see
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch05_ocean_mixing}).
\item \textbf{Vertical advection} $-\ww\,\partial_z C$ is upwind on the sign of the
cell-centred $\ww$, in the \emph{same advective form} as the horizontal term. Mixing
advective horizontal with flux-form vertical introduces a spurious $-C\,\partial_z
\ww$ source that blows the temperature up; keeping both advective avoids it. The
vertical CFL is tiny ($|\ww|\Delta t/\Delta z\sim 10^{-3}$), so explicit upwind is
comfortably stable.
\item \textbf{Vertical diffusion} uses the stability-dependent $\kappa_z$ of
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch05_ocean_mixing}, with zero flux through dry interfaces and the
floor.
\item \textbf{Surface forcing} $F_{\mathrm{surf}}$ enters only the top wet cell:
$F_\Temp = Q/(\densz c_p\Delta z_0)$ with $c_p=3985\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$,
a virtual-salt term for freshwater, and an optional subpolar freshwater
\emph{hosing} (Sverdrups) added directly so it bypasses the coupler's ocean-mean
balancing --- the bifurcation forcing of Chapter~\ref{chap:ch17_amoc_tipping}.
\end{itemize}

This tendency is integrated with the classical fourth-order Runge--Kutta scheme,
\begin{align}
  C^{n+1} &= C^{n} + \frac{\Delta t}{6}\,(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4),\\
  k_1 &= \mathcal{T}(C^n),\quad
  k_2 = \mathcal{T}\!\big(C^n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}k_1\big),\quad
  k_3 = \mathcal{T}\!\big(C^n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}k_2\big),\quad
  k_4 = \mathcal{T}\!\big(C^n+\Delta t\,k_3\big),
  \label{eq:rk4}
\end{align}
applied simultaneously to $(\Temp,\Salt,\mathrm{DIC})$, with every tendency frozen
to zero in dry cells. RK4 is fourth-order accurate and has a comfortable stability
region for the advection--diffusion operator at $\Delta t=900\,\mathrm{s}$; it is
also smooth, so reverse-mode AD through the whole step is clean.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{\code{tendencies}, \code{\_horiz}, \code{\_vadv}, \code{\_vdiff} + the RK4 combination}

\subsection{Post-step stabilisers, clips, and conservation}

After the RK4 update the model applies, in order: an optional biharmonic
($\nabla^4$) Shapiro filter (off by default, a light 2-grid-point cleanup; the
physical Laplacian diffusion is the primary noise control); a two-tier salinity
relaxation (a strong 6-month high-latitude sponge poleward of $65^\circ$ plus a weak
3-year global relaxation toward $35\,\mathrm{psu}$); hard physical clips
$\Salt\in[30,38]\,\mathrm{psu}$, $\Temp\in[250,320]\,\mathrm{K}$ and a
$271.35\,\mathrm{K}$ ($-1.8^\circ\mathrm{C}$) freezing floor standing in for absent
sea-ice latent heat; and finally a volume-weighted global-mean salinity
renormalisation back to $34.7\,\mathrm{psu}$,
\begin{equation}
  \Salt \;\leftarrow\; \Big(\Salt - \big(\langle\Salt\rangle_{\mathrm{wet}}
        - 34.7\big)\Big)\,m_C ,
\end{equation}
which removes slow numerical/brine drift as a uniform additive shift, leaving the
spatial \emph{gradient} structure (which drives the circulation) untouched. The
clips use \jax's straight-through subgradient: gradient $1$ in the interior, $0$ at
a boundary; in normal runs the clips are not hit, so the differentiability of the
step is preserved in practice.

\section{The conjugate-gradient elliptic solver}\label{sec:cg}

The barotropic streamfunction \eqref{eq:psi-rhs} (and, in
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}, the variable-coefficient vorticity
inversion) requires solving a large sparse linear system $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$
every step. Because the solver sits \emph{inside} the differentiated time step, it
must itself be differentiable --- ruling out \code{scipy.sparse.linalg.cg}. The
model uses a hand-written, \jax-native preconditioned conjugate gradient (CG).

For a symmetric positive-definite $A$, CG generates iterates that minimise the
$A$-norm of the error over an expanding Krylov subspace. With Jacobi (diagonal)
preconditioner $M\approx\operatorname{diag}(A)^{-1}$ the recurrence is
\begin{align}
  \alpha_k &= \frac{\mathbf{r}_k^{\!\top}\mathbf{z}_k}{\mathbf{p}_k^{\!\top}A\mathbf{p}_k},
  &\mathbf{x}_{k+1} &= \mathbf{x}_k + \alpha_k\mathbf{p}_k,
  &\mathbf{r}_{k+1} &= \mathbf{r}_k - \alpha_k A\mathbf{p}_k,\notag\\
  \mathbf{z}_{k+1} &= M\mathbf{r}_{k+1},
  &\beta_k &= \frac{\mathbf{r}_{k+1}^{\!\top}\mathbf{z}_{k+1}}{\mathbf{r}_k^{\!\top}\mathbf{z}_k},
  &\mathbf{p}_{k+1} &= \mathbf{z}_{k+1} + \beta_k\mathbf{p}_k.
  \label{eq:cg}
\end{align}

The implementation has two design choices worth highlighting:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Fixed-length \code{scan}, not \code{while\_loop}.} \jax's
\code{while\_loop} is not reverse-mode differentiable, so the loop runs a fixed
\code{max\_iter} steps via \code{jax.lax.scan}, and once the residual drops below
$\mathrm{tol}\cdot\|\mathbf{b}\|$ each further iteration is a no-op (the update is
masked back to the converged state with \code{jnp.where}). This keeps the solver
both differentiable and statically shaped.
\item \textbf{Matrix-free linear operator.} $A$ is never assembled; it is a function
$\mathbf{x}\mapsto A\mathbf{x}$. For the 2-D Poisson solve the operator is the
5-point Laplacian with periodic boundaries in $x$ (\code{jnp.roll}) and Dirichlet
$\psib=0$ in $y$ and on land. Land is handled by an \emph{identity-row} trick: on a
land cell the operator returns $\mathbf{x}$ itself, so $b=0$ there forces $\psib=0$
without making the matrix singular. The sign is flipped to $-\lap$ so $A$ is positive
definite, and the Jacobi diagonal is the constant $2/\Delta x^2 + 2/\Delta y^2$.
\end{itemize}
A short excerpt shows the differentiable masked update at the heart of the loop:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
def scan_body(state, _):
    x, r, p, rz_old, r_norm, it = state
    converged = r_norm <= tol_abs
    new_state = body_fun(state)          # one CG step (Eq. for CG)
    out = jax.tree.map(                   # freeze once converged -> no-op
        lambda old, new: jnp.where(converged, old, new), state, new_state)
    return out, None
\end{lstlisting}
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/solver.py}{\code{solve\_cg}, \code{solve\_poisson\_2d}, \code{jacobi\_preconditioner}}

\subsection{Variable-coefficient (JEBAR-ready) operators}\label{sec:varcoef}

The same CG kernel backs two further elliptic solvers built for the prognostic core
of Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}: a Cartesian
$\diver(c\,\grad\psib)=\text{rhs}$ and its spherical counterpart
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{\Erad^2\cos\lat}\Big[\pd{}{\lon}\Big(\frac{c}{\cos\lat}\,\pd{\psib}{\lon}\Big)
  + \pd{}{\lat}\Big(c\cos\lat\,\pd{\psib}{\lat}\Big)\Big] = \text{rhs}.
  \label{eq:sphere-elliptic}
\end{equation}
With $c=\dep^{-1}$ over real bathymetry this is precisely the topographic / JEBAR
operator a barotropic-vorticity ocean needs; with $c=\text{const}$ it reduces to the
Poisson solve above. The spherical version is discretised in symmetric
\emph{face-conductance} form (multiplying each cell equation by its area
$\Erad^2\cos\lat\,\Delta\lon\,\Delta\lat$) so the matrix stays SPD even as the cell
area varies with latitude, and a polar floor $\cos\lat\ge 0.087$ avoids the
$1/\cos\lat$ blow-up at the lat--lon pole singularity. Face coefficients are the
arithmetic mean of adjacent cells and are \emph{not} masked, so ocean cells couple to
land cells pinned to $\psib=0$ --- the correct Dirichlet streamfunction boundary
(masking the faces would impose Neumann and make the operator singular). Both solvers
are differentiable in $c$ and in the right-hand side.
\codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/solver.py}{\code{solve\_elliptic\_varcoef}, \code{solve\_elliptic\_varcoef\_sphere}}

\section{Honest summary of the numerics}

Tracers are the only prognostic ocean variables; they are advanced by RK4 on an
upwind-advection / Laplacian-diffusion operator that is carefully made
divergence-consistent and mass-conserving. The velocity that does the advecting is
\emph{diagnosed} each step from density (damped thermal wind) and wind stress
(flat-bottom Stommel streamfunction), then patched with a per-latitude net-transport
corrector and an explicit thermohaline overturning closure to give density a working
pathway to the AMOC. The diagnostic-velocity design is differentiable and stable, but
it is fundamentally limited: it has no momentum memory, no topographic vorticity
torque, and no self-consistent overturning. Removing these limitations is the job of
the prognostic-momentum core in Chapter~\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}, which
reuses the variable-coefficient CG solver of \S\ref{sec:varcoef}.

\begin{exercise}[Net-transport corrector]
Disable the per-latitude corrector \eqref{eq:corrector} (set the subtracted term to
zero in a copy of \code{step\_ocean}) and run a short spin-up. Diagnose the
zonally-/vertically-integrated meridional transport as a function of latitude and
confirm it reaches order $\pm 10^2\Sv$. Re-enable the corrector and verify it drops to
$\approx 0\Sv$. Why does a closed basin require zero net transport at every latitude?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Rigid-lid projection and tracer conservation]
Remove the depth-mean divergence subtraction in \eqref{eq:w-project} so that
$\ww_{\mathrm{if}}$ is integrated up from the floor without projection. Run a
multi-decade tracer-only experiment and measure the drift in global-mean $\Temp$.
Compare against the documented $\sim +0.25\ \mathrm{K\,yr^{-1}}$ leak. Explain why the
advective form of vertical advection (rather than flux form) makes this projection
necessary.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[CG convergence and warm starts]
Instrument \code{solve\_poisson\_2d} to record the residual norm versus iteration for
the barotropic solve. Compare convergence when starting from $\psib=0$ versus warm
starting from the previous step's $\psib$ (as \code{step\_ocean} does). How many
iterations does the warm start save once the model is near equilibrium, and why?
