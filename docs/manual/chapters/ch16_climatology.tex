The previous chapters built \model{} component by component: the ocean primitive
equations (Ch.~\ref{chap:ch04_ocean_equations}--\ref{chap:ch07_ocean_prognostic_core}),
the overturning closure (Ch.~\ref{chap:ch08_ocean_overturning}), the multi-level
\dino{} and single-level spectral atmospheres
(Ch.~\ref{chap:ch09_atmosphere_multilevel}--\ref{chap:ch10_atmosphere_singlelevel}),
and the bulk-flux coupler (Ch.~\ref{chap:ch13_coupler_fluxes}). A model that
runs, conserves, and stays finite has cleared only the lowest bar. The question
this Part~IV chapter asks is the harder one: \emph{is it right?} We answer it for
the time-mean state --- the \emph{climatology} --- by confronting the coupled
control run with two observational references and reporting area-weighted skill,
honestly, including where the model is wrong and why that matters.

The climatology is the cleanest target for a first evaluation because it
averages away weather and interannual noise: a sufficiently long time mean of a
stable run should reproduce the large-scale geographical patterns of
temperature, salinity, and pressure that a textbook climate atlas shows. It is
also the most unforgiving target, because every structural bias survives the
averaging. We proceed by (i) defining the diagnostic and its observational
references (\S\ref{sec:c16_framework}), (ii) stating the four area-weighted
metrics the validation framework computes (\S\ref{sec:c16_metrics}), (iii)
presenting the ocean and atmosphere bias-map and zonal-mean dashboards
(\S\ref{sec:c16_ocean}--\S\ref{sec:c16_atmos}), and (iv) reading the mean state
honestly --- the closed salt budget and realistic large-scale structure on the
one hand, the documented cold-tropics bias and its downstream consequence on the
other (\S\ref{sec:c16_honesty}).

\section{The diagnostic and its observational references}\label{sec:c16_framework}

\subsection{What a climatology is}

Let $X(\lon,\lat,t)$ be a model field. Its climatology is the time mean
%
\begin{equation}
  \overline{X}(\lon,\lat) \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T} X(\lon,\lat,t)\,\dd t ,
  \label{eq:c16_climmean}
\end{equation}
%
taken over an \emph{equilibrated} window $T$ long enough that the weather
spectrum and any residual spin-up transient are averaged out. In practice this
is a discrete mean over a glob of saved checkpoints. The dashboard harness
\codref{experiments/make\_readme\_figures.py}{averages a glob of saved states
into a climatology before scoring} explicitly averages a sequence of yearly
checkpoints into $\overline{X}$ so that weather noise and spin-up do not swamp
the time-mean signal. The quantitative scorecard reported below is refreshed
from the latest control mean; a 30-yr time mean of the coupled
\dino{}$\leftrightarrow$ocean control is being generated for the headline
numbers, and the figures in this chapter are the current validation dashboard.

\subsection{The two observational references}

\model{} is graded against two community reference datasets, each on its native
grid, loaded by \codref{chronos\_esm/validation/obs.py}{WOA18 and ERA5 loaders,
returning native-grid NumPy arrays}:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,style=nextline]
  \item[Ocean --- WOA18.] The World Ocean Atlas 2018 annual climatology supplies
  sea-surface temperature ($\Temp$, the topmost level) and sea-surface salinity
  ($\Salt$) \citep{locarnini2018woa,zweng2018woa}. WOA18 is also the dataset used to \emph{initialise} the ocean
  (Ch.~\ref{chap:ch04_ocean_equations}), so the surface-temperature comparison
  must be read with care: in the flux-corrected control the surface heat flux
  \emph{restores} SST toward WOA (Haney restoring \citep{haney1971}; Ch.~\ref{chap:ch13_coupler_fluxes}),
  so SST skill is partly imposed, not predicted. The genuinely emergent ocean
  field is salinity, which evolves freely.
  \item[Atmosphere --- ERA5.] The ECMWF ERA5 reanalysis \citep{hersbach2020era5} (monthly means,
  averaged to an annual climatology) supplies 2~m air temperature ($\Tair$),
  10~m zonal and meridional winds ($\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}},\vv_{\mathrm{sfc}}$),
  total precipitation, and mean sea-level pressure (\code{mslp}). ERA5 is an
  independent reference --- the model is never initialised from it --- so every
  atmospheric metric is an honest skill score.
\end{description}

Both references are regridded onto the model's T31 grid before scoring
(\code{grid.regrid\_to\_model}), and each field is converted to a human-friendly
display unit (precipitation in mm/day, pressure in hPa) by the per-variable
display table in \codref{chronos\_esm/validation/scorecard.py}{the \_DISPLAY
table maps each field to units, scale, colour map}. The canonical model fields
the scorecard reads are \code{sst}, \code{sss}, \code{t2m}, \code{u\_sfc},
\code{v\_sfc}, \code{precip}, and \code{mslp}.

\section{Area-weighted skill metrics}\label{sec:c16_metrics}

All metrics are area weighted. On a latitude--longitude grid the cells shrink
toward the poles, so each cell carries the weight $\wcell \propto \cos\lat_i$
(\code{grid.area\_weights}); a cell contributes only where \emph{both} model and
observation are finite, which masks land for ocean fields automatically. Writing
$\langle\cdot\rangle$ for the area-weighted average over valid cells, the four
diagnostics in \codref{chronos\_esm/validation/metrics.py}{area\_weighted\_stats
and taylor\_stats compute bias, RMSE, pattern correlation, and the std ratio}
are:

\begin{align}
  \text{bias} \quad
    &\bias = \langle X_{\mathrm{mod}}\rangle - \langle X_{\mathrm{obs}}\rangle ,
    \label{eq:c16_bias}\\[2pt]
  \text{RMSE} \quad
    &\rmse = \sqrt{\big\langle (X_{\mathrm{mod}} - X_{\mathrm{obs}})^2 \big\rangle} ,
    \label{eq:c16_rmse}\\[2pt]
  \text{pattern correlation} \quad
    &\corrp = \frac{\big\langle X_{\mathrm{mod}}' \, X_{\mathrm{obs}}' \big\rangle}
                   {\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}\,\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}} ,
    \label{eq:c16_corr}\\[2pt]
  \text{std ratio} \quad
    &\stdr = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{mod}}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}} ,
    \label{eq:c16_stdr}
\end{align}
%
where a prime denotes the deviation from the area-weighted mean,
$X' = X - \langle X\rangle$, and $\sigma^2 = \langle X'^2\rangle$. These four
separate the kinds of error a climatology can have. The \emph{bias}
(Eq.~\ref{eq:c16_bias}) is the global offset --- is the model on average too warm
or too salty? The \emph{RMSE} (Eq.~\ref{eq:c16_rmse}) is the total point-by-point
error, conflating offset and pattern. The \emph{pattern correlation}
(Eq.~\ref{eq:c16_corr}) removes the mean from each field and asks only whether
the model puts the highs and lows in the \emph{right places}; $\corrp=1$ is a
perfect spatial pattern regardless of amplitude. The \emph{std ratio}
(Eq.~\ref{eq:c16_stdr}) measures whether the spatial \emph{amplitude} is right:
$\stdr<1$ means the model is too smooth (too little spatial contrast),
$\stdr>1$ means it is too noisy or too sharp. A perfect model sits at
$\bias=0$, $\corrp=1$, $\stdr=1$. The pair $(\corrp,\stdr)$ are the polar
coordinates of a Taylor diagram \citep{taylor2001}, which the framework also
emits, and they are exactly the right summary for a climatology because they are
insensitive to a constant offset that a flux correction may impose.

Two reductions present the spatial detail. The \emph{bias map}
$\overline{X}_{\mathrm{mod}} - \overline{X}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ shows \emph{where}
the model is wrong, on a diverging colour scale centred at zero. The
\emph{zonal mean} $\langle X\rangle_\lon(\lat)$, the longitude average per
latitude band (\code{metrics.zonal\_mean}), collapses the field to a single
latitude profile and is the cleanest view of the large-scale meridional
structure that the primitive equations are supposed to set.

\section{Ocean climatology versus WOA18}\label{sec:c16_ocean}

\subsection{Sea-surface temperature}

Figure~\ref{fig:c16_biasmap_sst} maps the SST bias and
Fig.~\ref{fig:c16_zonal_sst} the SST zonal mean against WOA18. The model
reproduces the canonical meridional SST structure --- a warm equatorial belt,
the subtropical maxima, and the steep poleward cooling --- with a near-unity
pattern correlation and std ratio, as expected for a field whose surface is
restored toward the same climatology in the flux-corrected control. The honest
reading is therefore that SST is largely \emph{imposed} at the surface, not
predicted; its value here is as a consistency check (the restoring is doing its
job and the run is stable) rather than as an independent skill claim. The
informative residual is the spatial pattern of the small remaining bias, which
the bias map exposes.

\subsection{Sea-surface salinity}

Salinity is the emergent ocean test, because nothing restores it to WOA at the
surface in the modern control --- the global \emph{salt budget is closed} (water
conservation plus volume-mean salinity conservation), so the model no longer
drifts its salinity into the physics-stability clip
(Ch.~\ref{chap:ch05_ocean_mixing}; \codref{chronos\_esm/ocean/veros\_driver.py}{
high-latitude salinity sponge and the closed volume-mean salt budget}). The
zonal-mean salinity (Fig.~\ref{fig:c16_zonal_sss}) reproduces the textbook
double-peaked structure --- saline subtropical gyres flanking a fresher
equatorial band, with fresh high latitudes --- which is the signature of the
evaporation-minus-precipitation pattern that the freshwater flux imposes. The
bias map (Fig.~\ref{fig:c16_biasmap_sss}) shows the pattern is right where the
amplitude is honestly under-resolved: at T31 the std ratio is below one (the
spatial salinity contrast is too smooth), and the pattern correlation, while
positive in the forced-atmosphere assessment, is modest. The key honest point is
conservation: the \emph{global-mean} salinity bias is small precisely because the
budget is closed, and the remaining error is a pattern/amplitude deficiency, not
a runaway drift.

\section{Atmosphere climatology versus ERA5}\label{sec:c16_atmos}

\subsection{2 m air temperature}

The 2~m air temperature (Figs.~\ref{fig:c16_biasmap_t2m},
\ref{fig:c16_zonal_t2m}) is the atmosphere's thermodynamic mean state. Its
large-scale meridional structure --- warm tropics, cold poles, the land/ocean
contrast --- follows the SST it sits over, so $\Tair$ inherits both the strength
and the weakness of the surface ocean. In the flux-corrected coupled control the
$\Tair$ pattern correlation against ERA5 is high (the documented control
scorecard reports $\corrp\approx0.88$ once the continents are given an
interactive land surface and no longer behave as a uniformly cold ocean), but
the field carries a documented warm bias and an over-damped spatial amplitude:
the control std ratio is below one, meaning the model spreads too little
warm--cold contrast across the globe. Because the SST-driven part of $\Tair$ is
imposed by the SST restoring, the honestly \emph{emergent} skill here is the
land/ocean contrast and the continental pattern, not the absolute temperature.

  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{biasmap_t2m}

\subsection{Mean sea-level pressure --- the weakest field}

Mean sea-level pressure (Fig.~\ref{fig:c16_biasmap_mslp}) is, by design, the
field on which \model{}'s atmosphere is graded most harshly, and it is the
weakest field in the dashboard. The two atmospheric options expose two distinct
failure modes, both documented and both understood. The default
\emph{single-level barotropic} atmosphere (Ch.~\ref{chap:ch10_atmosphere_singlelevel})
has no baroclinic eddies, so it cannot generate the synoptic highs and lows that
organise the observed pressure field; its \code{mslp} \emph{pattern} correlation
stays near zero. Crucially, this was hard-won: an earlier polar instability ---
the lat--lon $\dd x$ collapsed at the poles and detonated the
$R\,\Temp\,\lap(\ln \pres_s)$ term --- had pinned the pressure field to its clamp
with a variance $\sim\!215\times$ observed, until the dynamical metric was
floored, bringing the variance to $\sim\!2\times$ observed (a physical, if
pattern-poor, field). The multi-level \dino{} atmosphere
(Ch.~\ref{chap:ch09_atmosphere_multilevel}) \emph{does} perform genuine
baroclinic instability \citep{kochkov2024neuralgcm}, yet its surface
pressure/wind \emph{pattern} skill is still not improved in the coupled control
(the documented control \code{mslp} correlation is negative): at T31 the
regridded WOA SST has only a $\sim\!22.6$~K equator--pole gradient, too weak for
the resolved eddy momentum flux to build the observed mid-latitude surface
pressure and wind pattern. The bias map should therefore be read as a
\emph{diagnosis of a known structural limit}, not a tuning failure: closing it
needs a sharper SST gradient (higher resolution) or an explicit
eddy-momentum parameterization.

  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{biasmap_mslp}
  \caption{Mean sea-level-pressure bias, $\overline{\pres}_{s,\mathrm{mod}}-

\end{figure}

\section{Honest assessment of the mean state}\label{sec:c16_honesty}

\subsection{What is right}

Three results in the dashboard are genuine, not artefacts of restoring. First,
the \emph{salt budget is closed}: salinity no longer drifts into the clip, the
global-mean bias is small, and the zonal-mean S structure is realistic --- this
is a conservation property of the discretisation
(Ch.~\ref{chap:ch06_ocean_numerics}), and it is a non-trivial thing for a free
ocean to get right. Second, the \emph{large-scale T/S structure} is correct: the
warm-tropics/cold-poles temperature profile and the double-peaked salinity
profile both emerge with high pattern correlation, confirming that the primitive
equations plus the surface forcing set the right meridional skeleton
\citep{vallis2017,griffies2004}. Third, when the continents are given an
interactive land surface, the \emph{land/ocean temperature contrast} is captured
($\Tair$ pattern correlation rises sharply in the documented control), and a
\emph{revived ITCZ} appears in precipitation once the run rains on the equator
rather than the subtropics --- the genuinely emergent atmospheric skill.

\subsection{The cold-tropics bias and its consequence}

The single most consequential mean-state error is a \emph{cold-tropics bias}: a
cold-tongue deficit in tropical SST relative to WOA, visible as the flattened
tropical maximum in the SST and $\Tair$ zonal means
(Figs.~\ref{fig:c16_zonal_sst}, \ref{fig:c16_zonal_t2m}). It arises from a
slow, basin-wide ocean cooling tendency that the surface flux balance pins at the
\emph{global mean} but not in its \emph{spatial pattern}: removing the global-mean
net heat keeps the planet's energy budget closed without fixing where the heat
is missing, so the tropics run cold while the global mean stays on target. A
related subtlety surfaced and was fixed in the coupled control --- the heat-flux
adjustment had been balanced over land$+$ocean cells, leaving a residual net
\emph{ocean} cooling because heat is applied only to ocean cells; balancing the
adjustment over ocean cells only flattened the SST drift (documented: a
$17.3\to14.9\,^\circ$C drift became $17.3\to17.5\,^\circ$C, flat).

The consequence is real and runs downstream into every other diagnostic. A cold,
flat tropical SST means a weak equator--pole and equator--subtropics SST
gradient, which (i) weakens the Hadley overturning and dries the ITCZ in the
single-level atmosphere, leaving precipitation too dry, and (ii) deprives the
multi-level \dino{} atmosphere of the baroclinicity it needs to build
mid-latitude surface westerlies and a realistic pressure pattern --- exactly the
\code{mslp} weakness of \S\ref{sec:c16_atmos}. It is also why the
CO$_2$ response of Ch.~\ref{chap:ch14_forcing_response} must be read as a
transient surface-forcing proxy on a cold base state rather than an equilibrium
sensitivity. The mean-state cold-tropics bias is thus not a cosmetic blemish; it
is the upstream limiter on the model's hydrological and dynamical realism, and it
is the next calibration target. The differentiable framework of
Ch.~\ref{chap:ch15_differentiable_applications} is the intended lever for
attacking it: a gradient-based fit of the surface flux and mixing parameters to
the WOA/ERA5 climatology is exactly the kind of high-dimensional calibration
that automatic differentiation makes tractable \citep{jax2018}.

\subsection{Reading the scorecard}

The quantitative scorecard --- one row per field with bias, RMSE, pattern
correlation, std ratio, and the count of valid cells --- is printed by
\code{scorecard.format\_scorecard} and refreshed from the latest 30-yr control
mean as new checkpoints accumulate. Read it field by field with the metric
roles of \S\ref{sec:c16_metrics} in mind: trust $\corrp$ and $\stdr$ over
$\bias$ wherever a flux correction can impose the mean (SST, the SST-driven
part of $\Tair$); treat salinity, precipitation, and the land/ocean contrast as
the emergent skill; and read \code{mslp} and the meridional wind as the
documented structural ceiling of a single-gradient T31 atmosphere. The figures
in this chapter are the current validation dashboard against which the next
generation of calibrated runs will be measured.

\begin{remark}
A perfect score on a flux-corrected control is not a goal; it would only mean the
restoring is strong. The scientific content of a climatology evaluation lives in
the \emph{residuals} that the restoring cannot reach --- the salinity pattern,
the precipitation, the pressure field --- and in being explicit about which
numbers are imposed and which are earned.
\end{remark}

\section*{Exercises: the model as laboratory}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Exercises}

\begin{exercise}[Restored versus emergent skill]
Run the validation harness on a saved control climatology and record the
scorecard for \code{sst} and \code{sss}. Now turn off the SST restoring (set the
Haney coefficient $\to0$; Ch.~\ref{chap:ch13_coupler_fluxes}), re-run a
short equilibration, and re-score. Quantify how much of the SST pattern
correlation collapses without restoring, and confirm that the salinity score is
essentially unchanged. Explain, using Eqs.~\eqref{eq:c16_corr}--\eqref{eq:c16_stdr},
why the std ratio is a more honest summary than the bias for a restored field.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Diagnosing the cold-tropics bias]
From the SST and $\Tair$ zonal means (Figs.~\ref{fig:c16_zonal_sst},
\ref{fig:c16_zonal_t2m}), extract the model's equator-to-pole temperature
gradient and compare it to the $\sim\!22.6$~K WOA value documented for the T31
grid. Then plot the precipitation zonal mean and test the hypothesis that a
weaker tropical SST gradient produces a drier, less equatorially-peaked ITCZ.
Discuss why fixing the global-mean energy balance does not, by itself, fix the
tropical pattern.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Calibrating against the climatology]
Using the differentiable pipeline of Ch.~\ref{chap:ch15_differentiable_applications},
define the area-weighted RMSE of \eqref{eq:c16_rmse} between the model SST
climatology and WOA18 as a scalar cost. Compute its gradient with respect to a
vertical-mixing or surface-flux parameter and take a few descent steps. Report
whether the tropical cold bias and the global-mean energy balance can be improved
\emph{simultaneously}, and reflect on whether a single scalar parameter is enough
or whether a spatial pattern of corrections is required.
\end{exercise}
