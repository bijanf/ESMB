\chapter{Mid-Latitude Jets and Storm Tracks}
\label{chap:ch18_jets_stormtracks}

The mid-latitude westerly jets and the storm tracks that flank them are among the most
demanding tests of an atmospheric model. They are not directly forced --- there is no external
agent that blows the wind eastward at $40^\circ$ latitude. Instead they are an \emph{emergent}
consequence of how the atmosphere disposes of the equator-to-pole temperature gradient. This
chapter explains the phenomenon, shows how \model{} represents it with two very different
atmospheres (the multi-level \dino{} dycore of
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch09_atmosphere_multilevel} and the single-level barotropic model of
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch10_atmosphere_singlelevel}), and presents the validation dashboard for the
surface winds --- honestly, including where the coarse T31 resolution defeats the dynamics.

\section{The phenomenon: baroclinic instability and the eddy-driven jet}

A rotating, differentially heated atmosphere is in approximate thermal-wind balance: the
poleward temperature gradient $\partial\Temp/\partial\lat$ is tied to a vertical shear of the
zonal wind,
\begin{equation}
  f\,\pd{\uu}{\dep} \;\approx\; -\frac{\grav}{\Temp}\,\pd{\Temp}{\lat},
  \label{eq:thermalwind}
\end{equation}
so the gradient alone produces westerlies that increase with height --- a \emph{thermal-wind
jet}. This balanced state is, however, baroclinically \emph{unstable}: small perturbations tap
the available potential energy stored in the sloping isentropes and grow into the travelling
synoptic eddies we call weather systems \citep{vallis2017}. These eddies are not noise on top
of the circulation; they \emph{are} the circulation. Their poleward heat flux relaxes the
gradient that feeds them, and --- crucially for the jet --- their momentum flux converges in
mid-latitudes, accelerating the surface westerlies. The mature state is therefore an
\emph{eddy-driven jet} sitting over a storm track, with a surface-wind tripole: trade
easterlies in the tropics, westerlies in mid-latitudes, and weak polar easterlies. No
parameterization puts the westerlies there; the eddies earn them.

The key implication for modelling is structural: \emph{baroclinic instability requires vertical
structure}. Equation~\eqref{eq:thermalwind} is a statement about shear, and a single-level
model has no shear to be unstable to. This single fact divides \model{}'s two atmospheres.

\section{Two representations in \model{}}

\subsection{The multi-level \dino{} dycore: eddies as physics}

\model{}'s multi-level atmosphere (Chapter~\ref{chap:ch09_atmosphere_multilevel}) is built on
Google's differentiable \dino{} spectral primitive-equation dycore --- the dynamical core
behind NeuralGCM \citep{kochkov2024neuralgcm}. Run at T31 with 24 $\sigma$-levels, an
ImEx--RK3 integrator and a $\nabla^4$ hyperdiffusion filter, it resolves vertical shear and
therefore performs \emph{genuine} baroclinic instability: it grows transient eddies and builds
an eddy-driven jet from the internal dynamics, with no jet prescribed.

The canonical proof is the dry Held--Suarez test \citep{heldsuarez1994}, in which the only
forcing is Newtonian relaxation toward a prescribed radiative-equilibrium temperature plus
boundary-layer Rayleigh drag. Started from an isothermal rest state, the \dino{} core in
\model{} reproduces the textbook circulation: a pair of upper-tropospheric mid-latitude
westerly jets of $\sim$22~m/s and surface trade easterlies (Figure~\ref{fig:hs_jet}). This is
exactly the structure the single-level model can never generate, and it confirms the dycore is
the right tool.

When this same core is coupled to the ocean SST (Chapter~\ref{chap:ch13_coupler_fluxes}), the
storm track appears. To make the eddies grow on the right timescale at coarse T31, the
initialization is anchored near radiative equilibrium (so the baroclinic jet exists from day
zero rather than after a two-month relaxation), a small rotational vorticity seed breaks the
exact zonal symmetry that an axisymmetric state would otherwise preserve indefinitely, and the
hyperdiffusion is weakened (relaxation timescale $2\!\to\!6$~h) so the $l\!\sim\!10$--$15$
eddies that carry the momentum flux are not damped before they grow. With these in place the
eddies fire within $\sim$2--6 weeks and the atmosphere develops a $\sim$27~m/s eddy-driven jet,
surface winds with visible synoptic structure, and an equatorial ITCZ
(Figure~\ref{fig:dino_circ}).

\subsection{The single-level model: a relaxed jet}

The default coupled atmosphere is single-level and barotropic
(Chapter~\ref{chap:ch10_atmosphere_singlelevel}). Having no vertical shear, it cannot satisfy
the thermal-wind relation~\eqref{eq:thermalwind} and cannot be baroclinically unstable, so left
to itself the winds geostrophically adjust to a near-standstill (and, before the v3 overhaul,
even \emph{anti}-correlated with ERA5). \model{} therefore \emph{parameterizes} the
eddy-momentum flux that the level cannot resolve: the winds are relaxed (timescale $\sim$2 days)
toward an observed zonal-mean surface-wind profile --- tropical easterlies, mid-latitude
westerlies, weak polar easterlies. This is an honest stand-in, not a derivation: it imposes the
\emph{climatological} jet rather than growing it. The decoupling is deliberate --- relaxation
sets the realism while strong hyperdiffusion provides stability --- and is discussed in
Chapter~\ref{chap:ch10_atmosphere_singlelevel}.

\section{Diagnostic and metric}

The jet/storm-track diagnostic is built from the near-surface zonal wind $\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}}$,
the meridional wind $\vv_{\mathrm{sfc}}$, and the mean sea-level pressure $\pres$. We score the
time-mean fields against ERA5 with the validation framework
(Chapter~\ref{chap:ch16_climatology}), reporting area-weighted bias, RMSE, the spatial pattern
correlation, and the standard-deviation ratio (a perfect model scores bias~0, corr~1, std
ratio~1). The \emph{zonal mean} of $\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}}$ versus latitude isolates the jet
structure --- the trade-westerly-polar tripole --- while the $\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}}$ bias map
exposes where the jet is misplaced. The MSLP zonal mean diagnoses the subpolar lows and
subtropical highs that the storm track organises.

\paragraph{Single-level (forced spin-up).} The relaxation parameterization succeeds at what it
is designed to do: the surface zonal-wind \emph{zonal mean} matches ERA5's tripole well and the
$\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}}$ pattern correlation reaches $\approx 0.72$ (recovered from $-0.08$ before
the overhaul). But this is the jet \emph{prescribed}, not earned, and the limits show in the
fields the relaxation does not set: the meridional wind has near-zero pattern correlation and
the MSLP pattern correlation stays near zero, because synoptic systems --- the eddies that
organise the storm track in two dimensions --- are simply absent.

\paragraph{Multi-level (SST-forced).} The \dino{} atmosphere earns its winds dynamically and
wins the thermodynamics/hydrology decisively (precipitation pattern correlation
$0.16\!\to\!0.34$ with a real ITCZ, plus a better near-surface temperature). On the surface
\emph{wind/pressure pattern}, however, it does \emph{not} yet beat the prescribed single-level
field, for a physically understood reason rather than a bug.

\paragraph{The honest limitation: a weak T31 SST gradient.} The eddy momentum flux that builds
the mid-latitude surface westerlies scales with the equator-to-pole temperature gradient. At
T31 the regridded WOA SST has only a $\sim$22.6~K equator--pole contrast --- the sharp Gulf
Stream, Kuroshio and ACC fronts are smoothed away --- roughly half the $\sim$45~K of an
idealized aquaplanet at which the textbook surface tripole emerges cleanly. At this weak
gradient the resolved eddy momentum flux is too small to build a clean mid-latitude
surface-westerly belt; a 22.6~K aquaplanet stays surface-easterly at mid-latitudes too, so this
is the gradient, not the orography. Consequently the \dino{} surface dynamic-field pattern
correlations stay near zero ($\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}}\approx-0.01$, with the coupled control near
$+0.10$), and the MSLP pattern correlation is negative. Recovering the observed surface
westerlies needs higher resolution (to resolve the SST fronts), an explicit eddy-momentum /
surface-stress parameterization, or realistic stationary-wave forcing (monsoons, land contrast)
that the Held--Suarez plus bulk-moisture physics does not yet supply. This is why the
single-level relaxed jet remains the \emph{default} coupled atmosphere: it trades dynamical
honesty for a usable surface-wind climatology.

\paragraph{Status of the scorecard.} The quantitative numbers quoted above are the documented
benchmark values; the headline surface-wind/pressure scorecard is being refreshed from the
latest control run (a 30-year time mean is being generated), since the dense transient eddies
otherwise swamp the weak time mean in a short climatology. The figures here are the current
validation dashboard for the jets and storm tracks.

\section{Summary}

\model{} represents the eddy-driven jet two ways. The multi-level \dino{} dycore does the
\emph{physics} --- baroclinic instability, transient storm-track eddies, an emergent
$\sim$27~m/s jet, validated against the Held--Suarez benchmark --- but at T31 the SST gradient
is too weak for those eddies to build realistic surface westerlies. The single-level model
\emph{parameterizes} the result, reaching a good surface-wind zonal mean ($\uu_{\mathrm{sfc}}$
corr $\approx 0.72$) at the cost of having no genuine synoptic systems (near-zero MSLP and
$\vv_{\mathrm{sfc}}$ pattern skill). Both are honest about what they cannot do: the storm track
in two dimensions remains the frontier, awaiting higher resolution or explicit eddy
parameterization.
